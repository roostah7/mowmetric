How we calculate the price

No black box. Here's exactly what goes into every estimate — and why it's honest.

📐

Your actual lawn size

We measure the exact polygon you draw in acres (with square feet shown alongside for providers who price by area) using geodesic calculation — the same math satellites use. No lot-size guessing.

💼

Real local labor rates

We pull landscaping wage data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics for your state, so the price reflects what crews in your area actually earn.

⏱️

Honest time estimates

We assume a typical 2-person residential crew with standard equipment covering ~0.5 acres (~22,000 sq ft) per crew-hour, plus 12 minutes of setup and travel overhead per job.

💰

Fair profit margin built in

Our formula includes a 2.4× labor markup for equipment, fuel, insurance, overhead, and a reasonable profit — the industry-standard margin for well-run lawn care businesses.

🗺️

Minimum job floor

Every estimate has a $35 minimum floor because no crew will travel to your property for less — small yards cost more per acre than large ones.

🔓

Open methodology

Every input — wage data, crew productivity, margin, tier breakpoints — is documented on this page. No hidden multipliers. No surge pricing. No affiliate kickbacks baked into the number.

🎯

Calibrated to fair bids

Our benchmark targets roughly the 65th percentile of real local quotes — high enough that an insured, licensed crew can bid it honestly and stay profitable, low enough that a homeowner isn't overpaying. We sample and re-tune against real market quotes regularly.

Frequently asked questions

Is this price guaranteed?

No. MowMetric is an independent pricing benchmark — not a marketplace. The number you see is what a fair, typical bid should look like in your area based on BLS wage data and your actual measured lawn size. Any bid within roughly ±15% of this number is reasonable. Well above or below deserves questions.

Where does the wage data come from?

U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS), SOC code 37-3011 — Landscaping and Groundskeeping Workers. We pull state-level mean hourly wages, updated annually. Data as of the most recent BLS release is baked into this calculator.

How accurate is the satellite measurement?

Very — for the polygon you draw. We use geodesic area calculation (the same math GPS uses) so the acreage and square-footage readings are accurate within ~2% on a typical 0.18-acre lawn. Accuracy depends on how carefully you trace your actual mowable grass (exclude driveways, beds, mulch, etc.).

What does "get matched with local pros" actually do?

When you submit the match form, MowMetric shares your job details (address, measured size, frequency, contact info) with up to 3 vetted lawn care providers in your area. They contact you directly with firm quotes. You pick whoever you want — or nobody. There's no obligation and MowMetric never charges you.

What do you do with my data?

If you only use the calculator, we keep nothing identifiable — your address is used to center the map and is not stored. If you submit the "Get matched" form, we share your contact details with up to 3 vetted local providers so they can quote your job — this is how we make money, and we're paid a referral fee by the providers, not by you. We never share your information with data brokers, ad networks, or third parties unrelated to your service request. Full details in our Privacy Policy.

Why does a small lawn cost so much per acre?

Every job has fixed overhead — setup, travel, unloading, trim detail work — that doesn't scale down with size. A $35 minimum floor exists because no crew will drive to your property, unload equipment, and do the work for less. Big lawns get cheaper per acre because fixed costs are spread across more area. That's why our formula uses four pricing tiers.

Why does a one-time cut cost more than recurring service?

Recurring customers get a consistent, shorter cut that a crew can move through quickly — and the crew can route-stack nearby jobs for lower fuel and drive time. A one-time cut usually means taller grass and a crew driving out for a single job, so per-visit pricing is about 15% higher than a regular bi-weekly cadence. Weekly customers get a small discount on top because they're the most efficient route to service.
